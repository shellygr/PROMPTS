=== /question chat-certora-situation ===
You are a top notch CEO who brought companies with great potential to prosperity from the abyss. i need practical help. in my company there's a problem of liquid money not available. the reasons are stupid. we're making projects longer by a bit and not getting paid. second stupid reason is that finance is unable to follow up with completed tasks and we think some work was not even invoiced. and then there are delays in collection. our new ceo of 1 year _should_ have done better here rather than increase expenses imo. 
my cofounder and i are furious. the new ceo overall organizes it all but lacks the vision or the discipline. he's also rigid and not budging from his position and unable to take responsibility for mistakes, apologize, or see that he's in the wrong at all. he's not a negative person but hard to move from his position even if it's wrong. he also does not appreciate my cofounder who did some the very heavylifting in the company in terms of revenues and client relationship. we can make some cuts but the biggest issue is getting more revenues and operating more efficiently. i am in a technical position, building a product, and am in good terms with everyone. I want to save the company because it's worth saving and many people are very committed to the mission and the opportunities that lie ahead. what can you advise to me?

ANSWER FROM CHAT

You have three problems tangled together: **cash leakage**, **operational blindness**, and **leadership trust breakdown**. Do not try to solve them all through arguments with the CEO. Solve them through **facts, cadence, ownership, and cash discipline**.

The company may be saveable, but only if you and your cofounder move from “furious” to “surgical.”

## 1. Make this a cash war room, not a blame session

For the next 4–8 weeks, the company needs a weekly or twice-weekly **cash command meeting** with the CEO, finance, delivery/project owners, sales/account owners, you, and your cofounder.

The meeting should not be philosophical. It should answer only:

1. What work has been completed but not invoiced?
2. What has been invoiced but not collected?
3. What work is delayed and therefore delaying cash?
4. Which client/account owner is responsible for unblocking each payment?
5. What cash will arrive this week, next week, and this month?
6. What spending is frozen until cash stabilizes?

You need a single live tracker. Not a finance report three weeks later. A live, brutally simple sheet.

Columns:

| Client | Project | Contract value | Milestone | Work status | Invoice status | Amount billable | Amount invoiced | Amount collected | Owner | Blocker | Next action | Due date |
| ------ | ------: | -------------: | --------- | ----------- | -------------- | --------------: | --------------: | ---------------: | ----- | ------- | ----------- | -------- |

This is the first thing I would insist on.

Not “finance should improve.”
Not “CEO should have known.”
A visible operating system.

## 2. Do a 10-day revenue recovery sprint

You likely have hidden cash sitting in three buckets:

**Bucket A: completed work not invoiced**
This is the dumbest and most urgent leak. Someone needs to audit every active and recently completed project against contracts, milestones, timesheets, delivery records, and client approvals.

**Bucket B: invoiced work not collected**
For every overdue invoice, assign a human owner. Not “finance.” A named person. Often the best collector is not finance but the relationship owner.

**Bucket C: almost-complete work that needs a push**
Some projects may be 90–95% done but not billable because one deliverable, signoff, or acceptance criterion is missing. These should get executive attention immediately.

The goal of the sprint is not “improve finance.” The goal is:

> “In 10 business days, identify every dollar of completed, billable, uninvoiced, overdue, or blocked revenue, and assign collection actions.”

You and your cofounder can push for this without making it a coup against the CEO.

## 3. Stop accepting “projects are a bit longer” as normal

Small overruns kill cash. Especially if billing is milestone-based.

You need three operating rules:

**No project runs late invisibly.**
Any project that slips by more than, say, 5–10% in time or budget gets escalated.

**No free work without approval.**
If clients are asking for extra scope, change requests need to be priced, deferred, or explicitly approved as strategic.

**No delivery without billing trigger awareness.**
Every project owner should know: “What exactly unlocks the next invoice?”

This one is critical. A technical/product culture often focuses on finishing the work. A cash-disciplined company focuses on finishing the **billable milestone**.

## 4. Separate the CEO problem into performance, behavior, and role fit

Right now you and your cofounder are emotionally mixing several things:

* He increased expenses while cash discipline was weak.
* He did not fix basic invoicing/collection controls.
* He is rigid and defensive.
* He does not properly value your cofounder.
* He may organize things but lacks vision/discipline.

These may all be true. But if you confront him with all of them at once, he will become more defensive.

Instead, narrow it to objective governance:

> “The company has a cash conversion problem. We need a 30-day recovery plan with named owners, weekly metrics, spending controls, and visible accountability.”

Make him respond to that. Not to “you failed.”

If he refuses even that, the issue becomes clearer: it is no longer just competence. It is leadership obstruction.

## 5. Create a “CEO accountability frame” without humiliating him

You need him to either step up or reveal that he cannot.

I would say something like this in a private meeting:

> “We are not here to relitigate the past. But the company has a serious cash discipline problem: work is being completed without timely invoicing, collections are delayed, and project overruns are eating cash. This has to change immediately. We need a 30-day operating plan with a live cash tracker, named owners, spending controls, and weekly review. We want you to lead it. But it has to be measurable, and we need full transparency.”

That gives him a path to lead.

Then define success:

* 100% audit of completed/uninvoiced work.
* 100% list of overdue invoices with owners.
* Weekly cash forecast.
* Freeze or approval process for discretionary spend.
* Project-level billing milestone tracker.
* Clear collection targets for the next 30 days.
* No new hiring or major expenses without cash review.
* Weekly report to founders/board/shareholders, depending on your structure.

## 6. Your role: become the trusted operator, not the angry founder

You are in a powerful position because you are technical, respected, and on good terms with people. Use that.

Do not become the person who says, “The CEO is bad.”
Become the person who says:

> “Here is the system we need so good people can execute and cash stops leaking.”

You can help by building or owning the operating dashboard, connecting delivery status to billing milestones, and quietly getting project/account owners aligned.

You are not finance, but you can expose the missing interface between **delivery and cash**.

In many companies, finance does not actually know what is billable because delivery does not report it in a structured way. Delivery does not know what finance needs. Sales/client owners assume finance is chasing. Finance assumes account owners are chasing. Everyone thinks someone else owns it.

Kill that ambiguity.

## 7. Protect your cofounder’s value politically

Your cofounder did heavy revenue and client relationship work. If the CEO underappreciates that, this is dangerous. Revenue relationships are company oxygen.

But do not frame it as “you don’t appreciate him.” Frame it operationally:

> “For the recovery period, we need the strongest client relationships directly involved in collections, renewals, and expansion. That means [cofounder] needs to be fully empowered on key accounts.”

Make your cofounder’s role impossible to ignore because it is tied to cash outcomes.

## 8. Cut expenses, but do not confuse cutting with saving

Yes, make cuts. But cutting alone rarely saves a company whose core issue is revenue conversion.

I would divide expenses into four groups:

**Keep:** anything directly tied to delivery, revenue, collections, core product, or client retention.

**Pause:** nice-to-have initiatives, speculative hiring, tools, events, consultants, low-ROI marketing.

**Renegotiate:** vendors, contractors, payment terms, office costs, retainers.

**Cut:** projects or roles that do not contribute to revenue, delivery, product survival, or client success within the next 90–180 days.

But be careful: panic cuts can damage delivery and make cash worse. The central question is:

> “Does this expense help us collect, deliver, sell, retain, or build the product that matters?”

If no, freeze it.

## 9. Install metrics that make denial impossible

For the next quarter, I would track:

* Cash on hand.
* Weekly cash inflow.
* Weekly cash outflow.
* Weeks of runway.
* Completed but uninvoiced revenue.
* Invoiced but uncollected revenue.
* Overdue receivables by age: 0–30, 31–60, 61–90, 90+ days.
* Project gross margin.
* Projects slipping past planned delivery date.
* Revenue blocked by missing client signoff.
* New sales pipeline likely to close in 30/60/90 days.
* Renewal/expansion opportunities.

The CEO’s opinions matter less when the numbers are visible.

## 10. Decide privately what happens if he does not change

You and your cofounder need a private alignment conversation.

Not just venting. Decide:

* What minimum changes must happen in 30 days?
* What authority does the CEO currently have?
* Who can remove, constrain, or rebalance him?
* Is there a board?
* Are you majority owners?
* Can you create an executive committee?
* Can finance report weekly to founders/board?
* Can your cofounder take over revenue operations?
* Can someone else own cash operations?
* Is the CEO coachable enough to keep with tighter governance?
* If not, what is Plan B?

You do not need to decide today that he must go. But you do need to decide what behavior would make that unavoidable.

## 11. The conversation I would have with the CEO

Use a tone that is firm, not explosive.

> “We need to talk about cash discipline. The problem is not only market conditions or sales. We are leaking cash operationally: projects are extending, completed work is not always invoiced, and collections are delayed. That is unacceptable for a company at our stage.
>
> We want you to lead the fix, but we need a different operating cadence immediately. Within one week, we need a complete view of completed-but-uninvoiced work, overdue invoices, blocked milestones, collection owners, and expected cash by week. We also need spending controls until this stabilizes.
>
> This is not about blame. But it is about accountability. If we cannot get visibility and discipline around cash, we are putting the company at risk.”

Then stop talking. Let him respond.

If he argues, bring it back:

> “We can debate causes later. Right now we need the tracker, the owners, and the weekly cash plan.”

## 12. What I would do in the next 72 hours

First, align with your cofounder. Decide the message, the non-negotiables, and who will say what.

Second, request the cash/revenue recovery meeting. Include CEO, finance, delivery/project leads, account owners.

Third, create the tracker yourself if nobody else does. Sometimes leadership begins by creating the artifact everyone else should have created.

Fourth, identify the top 10 cash opportunities: biggest unpaid invoices, biggest uninvoiced completed work, biggest near-complete billable milestones.

Fifth, freeze discretionary spend until the company has a 13-week cash view.

Sixth, make client relationship owners responsible for collection support. Finance alone should not chase strategic clients in isolation.

## 13. The deeper truth

Your CEO may be a decent organizer but not the right wartime CEO. Wartime leadership requires:

* cash obsession,
* speed,
* humility,
* ability to admit reality,
* ability to cut or change course,
* respect for revenue generators,
* and discipline around execution.

If he cannot do those things, you either need to **surround him with controls**, **change his role**, or **replace him**.

But do not start with the replacement conversation unless you already have governance alignment. Start with a measurable recovery plan. That will either save him, expose him, or save the company despite him.

The immediate mission is simple:

> **Turn completed work into invoices, invoices into cash, delayed projects into billable milestones, and vague responsibility into named ownership.**

That is where the rescue begins.
